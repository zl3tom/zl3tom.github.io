Q Codes and Jargon

Back to Guides

Common Q codes, when they help, and when plain language is better.

Why Q codes exist

Q codes are short signals that save time, especially on CW, and they are also used on voice and digital. You only need a handful to understand most contacts.

The most useful Q codes

QTH  location
QRM  interference from other stations
QRN  noise
QSB  fading
QSY  change frequency
QRZ  who is calling me?
QSL  confirmation
QRP  low power
QRO  high power
QRT  stop transmitting

How they sound on voice

  • “QTH Christchurch” means “my location is Christchurch”.
  • “Lots of QRM” means “I have interference from other stations”.
  • “Let’s QSY” means “let’s change frequency”.

When plain language is better

  • On repeaters and local chats, plain language often sounds more natural.
  • When someone is new, explain instead of stacking jargon.
  • If copy is weak, simple words can be clearer than codes.

Common ham shorthand you will hear

Signal and operating
QSO (contact), RST (report), split (TX and RX on different frequencies), pileup (many callers), net (organised group), NCS (net control station), QRM (interference), QSY (move frequency).
Etiquette
“Break” (join or interrupt), “stand by” (wait), “again” (repeat), “no copy” (did not hear it), “73” (best regards).

Two rules that prevent mistakes

  • If you did not copy a callsign, say so, and ask again, do not guess.
  • If you are unsure what someone means, ask, most operators are happy to explain.
