A practical checklist for getting on air, sounding confident, and being easy to work. This is general advice and applies across HF, VHF, and most modes.

Before you transmit

  • Listen first. Make sure the frequency is clear and you are not interrupting an ongoing QSO.
  • Know your split. If a DX or net is running split, follow their instructions.
  • Set levels sensibly. On SSB keep audio clean, on digital avoid overdrive, on FM keep deviation normal.
  • Have your log ready. Time (UTC), band, mode, and callsign are the basics.

Calling CQ

Keep it simple and repeat your callsign clearly.

Example (SSB)
CQ CQ CQ, this is ZL3TOM, ZL3TOM calling CQ and standing by.

If nobody replies, wait 10 to 20 seconds, then call again. If conditions are busy, call less often and listen more.

Replying to a CQ

  • Give their callsign once, then yours once, slowly.
  • If pileups happen, say only your callsign and wait for the other station to pick you out.
Example
ZL3ABC, this is ZL3TOM.

The basic QSO flow

  1. Calls and confirmation, for example: “ZL3TOM you are 59 in Christchurch, name Thomas.”
  2. They return your report and details.
  3. Short chat if you like, then a clear sign-off.

Signal reports

On SSB you will usually hear “59” style reports, on CW or digital you may hear RST or a simple “good copy”. Be consistent and avoid exaggeration when signals are weak.

Phonetics and clarity

  • Use standard phonetics when asked, especially if conditions are poor.
  • Speak across the mic, not directly into it, this helps reduce pops and distortion.
  • Keep your pace steady, fast speech is hard to copy through noise.

Good on-air practice

  • Identify with your callsign regularly, and at the end of your over.
  • Leave pauses so others can break in, or so emergency traffic can call.
  • If you make a mistake, correct it briefly and move on.
  • Be welcoming to new operators, and keep the tone friendly.

Logging and confirmations

Log the essentials, time (UTC), band, mode, callsign, and a note if needed. If you use online confirmations, keep your log accurate so matches are easy. See the QSL page for how I confirm contacts.

Repeaters, nets, and linked systems

  • On repeaters, pause briefly before talking so links and courtesy tones can trigger.
  • During nets, follow the net control station, keep transmissions short, and wait to be called in.
  • On linked systems, remember you may be heard across a wide area, keep it clean and concise.

Digital modes

  • Keep your audio drive conservative, clean signals matter more than power.
  • Follow the usual calling frequencies and band plans for your region.
  • In automated modes, monitor the contact, and be ready to stop if you cause QRM.
