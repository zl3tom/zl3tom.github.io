Guides

Repeaters and Nets

Back to Guides

This guide explains how to use amateur radio repeaters and participate in nets confidently. It applies mainly to VHF and UHF, including linked systems such as AllStar and EchoLink.

Repeater basics

  • Always listen first to make sure the repeater is clear.
  • Key the microphone, pause for a second, then speak.
  • Identify with your callsign regularly and at the end of your transmission.
  • Leave pauses between overs so others can join or emergency traffic can call.

Monitoring and calling

Monitoring
ZL3TOM monitoring.
Calling a station
ZL3ABC, ZL3ABC, this is ZL3TOM.

If nobody replies, wait a bit and try once more. If the repeater is busy, keep transmissions short and allow others to use it.

Joining an ongoing conversation

  • Wait for a clear gap, then say your callsign once.
  • If invited in, keep your first over short.
  • If it sounds like a directed net or important traffic, stand by.
Example
ZL3TOM.

Courtesy tones and timeouts

  • Most repeaters have a timeout timer, long overs may be cut off.
  • The courtesy tone indicates when it is safe for the next station to transmit.
  • On linked systems, allow extra pauses to account for delay.

Linked systems

Linked systems connect repeaters and nodes over the internet. You may be heard across a wide region or country.

  • Keep language and topics appropriate for a wide audience.
  • Avoid kerchunking. If you key up accidentally, briefly identify.
  • Do not monopolise the link, many users share it.

What is a net?

A net is an organised on air gathering run by a net control station. Most nets are directed, meaning you transmit only when instructed.

Checking into a net

Example check in
Net control, ZL3TOM, Christchurch, no traffic.
  • Wait for net control to ask for check ins.
  • Give your callsign, location, and whether you have traffic.
  • If you miss something, wait for a pause and ask briefly.

Passing messages on nets

  • Write everything down before transmitting.
  • Read back important details to confirm accuracy.
  • Use phonetics for names, places, and numbers if copy is weak.

Quick do and do not list

Do
Listen first, leave pauses, identify clearly, keep overs short, be welcoming to new operators.
Do not
Kerchunk, talk over others, run long overs, guess callsigns, or treat shared systems as private channels.
